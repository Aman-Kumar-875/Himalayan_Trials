Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about trekking in the Himalayas

What makes Himalayan Trials different from other trekking companies?

Himalayan Trials stands out due to our deep local expertise, commitment to sustainable tourism, and personalized service. Our guides are all locals with extensive knowledge of the regions we trek in, and we maintain small group sizes to ensure a quality experience. We also reinvest a portion of our profits into local community projects.

What is the best time of year to trek in the Himalayas?

The best seasons for trekking in the Himalayas are pre-monsoon (March to May) and post-monsoon (September to November). During these months, the weather is generally stable with clear skies, offering spectacular mountain views. Winter treks (December to February) are possible on lower altitude routes, while the monsoon season (June to August) is generally not recommended due to rain, leeches, and poor visibility.

Do I need travel insurance for Himalayan treks?

Yes, comprehensive travel insurance is mandatory for all our treks. Your policy must cover emergency evacuation and repatriation up to the maximum altitude of your trek, as well as trip cancellation, medical expenses, and personal belongings. We recommend purchasing insurance that specifically covers trekking at high altitudes.

How large are your trekking groups?

We keep our groups small, with a maximum of 12 trekkers per group. This ensures personalized attention, better group dynamics, and a lower impact on the environment and local communities. For private treks, we can accommodate any group size.

How do I book a trek with Himalayan Trials?

Booking is simple: browse our packages, select your preferred trek, and click the "Book Now" button. Fill out the booking form with your details and preferred dates. We'll confirm availability within 24 hours. To secure your booking, a 20% deposit is required, with the balance due 30 days before departure.

What payment methods do you accept?

We accept credit/debit cards (Visa, MasterCard, American Express), PayPal, bank transfers, and Western Union. For card payments, a 3.5% processing fee applies. Bank transfers are our preferred method for larger amounts.

What is your cancellation policy?

Our cancellation policy is as follows:
- 60+ days before departure: Full refund minus $100 admin fee
- 30-59 days: 50% refund
- 15-29 days: 25% refund
- 0-14 days: No refund
We strongly recommend purchasing trip cancellation insurance to protect your investment.

Can I customize a trek to suit my preferences?

Absolutely! We specialize in customized treks tailored to your preferences, timeframe, and budget. Contact us with your requirements, and our experts will design a personalized itinerary for you. Custom treks can be arranged for individuals, families, or groups of any size.

How physically fit do I need to be for Himalayan treks?

The required fitness level depends on the trek difficulty. For moderate treks like Everest Base Camp or Annapurna Circuit, you should be able to walk 5-7 hours daily with a light daypack. We recommend a training program of cardio exercises, hiking, and strength training for 2-3 months before your trek. For easier treks, general good health and regular walking are sufficient.

What gear and equipment do I need to bring?

We provide a detailed packing list specific to your trek after booking. Essential items include good hiking boots, warm layers, a quality sleeping bag, and personal medications. Many items can be rented or purchased in Kathmandu or other starting points. Porters will carry your main bag (limited to 15kg), while you'll carry a daypack with essentials.

Do I need any special vaccinations?

While there are no mandatory vaccinations for Nepal, Bhutan, or Tibet, we recommend consulting your doctor or a travel clinic 2-3 months before your trip. Typically recommended vaccinations include Hepatitis A and B, Typhoid, and Tetanus. Depending on your itinerary and the season, other preventive measures might be advised.

How do I prevent altitude sickness?

Altitude sickness prevention is crucial for high-altitude treks. Our itineraries include proper acclimatization days. Additionally:
- Ascend slowly and follow the guide's pace
- Stay hydrated (3-4 liters daily)
- Avoid alcohol and tobacco
- Consider preventive medication like Diamox (consult your doctor)
- Inform your guide immediately if you experience symptoms
Our guides are trained in recognizing and managing altitude sickness.

What is a typical day like on the trek?

A typical trekking day starts with breakfast around 7:00 AM, followed by 5-7 hours of walking with regular breaks. We usually reach our destination by mid-afternoon, giving you time to rest, explore the village, or simply enjoy the views. Dinner is served around 6:00-7:00 PM, and most trekkers retire early to prepare for the next day.

Will there be access to electricity and WiFi during the trek?

Most teahouses on popular routes have electricity, though it may be limited or solar-powered. Charging facilities are usually available for a small fee. WiFi is available in many lodges on main trekking routes like Everest and Annapurna, but the connection can be slow and unreliable. For remote treks, connectivity is limited. We recommend bringing a power bank and considering a local SIM card with data.

What permits do I need for trekking in the Himalayas?

Required permits vary by destination. In Nepal, most treks require a TIMS card and conservation area or national park permits. Bhutan requires a government-approved tour package with a daily tariff. Tibet treks need Chinese visas and special permits. All necessary permits are included in our packages, and we handle the paperwork. You'll just need to provide passport details and photos.

Is it safe to trek solo in the Himalayas?

While popular routes like Everest Base Camp and Annapurna Circuit can be trekked independently, we strongly recommend hiring at least a guide for safety, cultural insights, and logistical support. Solo trekking in remote areas carries significant risks, including navigation challenges, altitude sickness, and emergency response difficulties. Some regions, like Upper Mustang or Bhutan, legally require guides. Even experienced trekkers benefit from local expertise.

What type of accommodation can I expect during the trek?

On most treks, accommodation is in teahouses or mountain lodges - simple, family-run establishments offering basic but clean rooms. Typically, rooms have twin beds with mattresses, pillows, and blankets (we recommend bringing a sleeping bag for hygiene and extra warmth). Bathroom facilities are shared, and hot showers (where available) usually cost extra. In remote areas, facilities are more basic. Premium packages offer the best available rooms at each stop.

What kind of food is available during treks?

Teahouses offer surprisingly varied menus, with the staple being "Dal Bhat" (rice, lentil soup, and vegetables) - a nutritious, all-you-can-eat option favored by guides and porters. Other common items include noodles, pasta, potatoes, soups, momos (dumplings), and simple Western dishes. Breakfast options typically include porridge, eggs, pancakes, and toast. Food is prepared fresh but menu variety decreases at higher altitudes. Vegetarian options are always available.

Is the drinking water safe during the trek?

We advise against drinking untreated tap or stream water. Safe drinking water is available through:
- Boiled water from teahouses (small fee)
- Purification tablets or SteriPEN (bring your own)
- Water filters (some lodges have filtered water stations)
- Bottled water (available but not environmentally friendly)
We encourage using refillable bottles with purification methods to reduce plastic waste in the mountains.

Are there any luxury trekking options available?

Yes, we offer luxury trekking packages on select routes, featuring the best available accommodations (premium lodges or luxury tents), enhanced meal options, more comfortable transportation, and additional services like massage treatments where available. These packages also include extras such as higher guide-to-client ratios, more comprehensive first aid kits, and satellite phones. Luxury options are most developed in the Everest and Annapurna regions.
